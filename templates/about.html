About This Project

Research-based software implementation of a B.Sc. Computer Science thesis at Caleb University, Lagos.

Research Problem

Nigerian e-commerce has grown rapidly between 2020 and 2026, driven by platforms like Jumia and Konga. However, last-mile delivery infrastructure has not kept pace. The result is a persistent pattern of delivery failures, delays, and SLA breaches — estimated at 25% delays, 15% failures, and 35% SLA breaches nationally.

This software implements a predictive system that classifies delivery outcomes before the rider leaves the depot, enabling logistics operators to intervene proactively rather than reactively.

Research Objectives

  • Identify key operational and environmental variables influencing delivery outcomes in Nigerian e-commerce
  • Develop and compare three supervised classification models: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, and Random Forest
  • Evaluate model performance using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and confusion matrices
  • Identify the most predictive features via feature importance analysis
  • Implement a working prediction system for operational use

Dataset

The dataset consists of 10,000 synthetic delivery records generated using parameters calibrated to Nigerian logistics literature (2020–2026). It combines characteristics from public datasets with simulation techniques to reflect real-world conditions including Lagos traffic patterns, informal addressing systems, seasonal flooding, and motorcycle-dependent fleets.

Methodology

The research follows a five-phase methodology: data collection and generation, preprocessing and feature engineering, exploratory data analysis, model development with hyperparameter tuning, and evaluation. Data is split 70% training, 15% validation, 15% test with stratified sampling to maintain class distribution. GridSearchCV with 5-fold stratified cross-validation is used for hyperparameter optimisation, with F1-macro as the scoring metric to account for class imbalance.
